# Interview Day Protocol — The Night Before & Morning Of

## The Night Before

### Do
- [ ] Review your STAR stories (just read, don't memorize)
- [ ] Quick scan of DSA cheatsheet (patterns, not problems)
- [ ] Confirm interview time, link, and interviewer names
- [ ] Prepare your environment (quiet room, good lighting, charged laptop)
- [ ] Set up IDE/editor (for coding rounds)
- [ ] Have water, pen, paper ready
- [ ] Sleep by 10:30 PM — 7+ hours is non-negotiable

### Don't
- [ ] Don't solve new hard problems (it'll shake confidence)
- [ ] Don't stay up late "cramming"
- [ ] Don't read interview horror stories online
- [ ] Don't try to predict exact questions

---

## Morning Of

### 2 Hours Before
- Eat a good breakfast (protein + complex carbs, not sugar)
- Light exercise or walk (10 min — reduces anxiety)
- Review ONE thing you're most nervous about (just to feel prepared)

### 30 Minutes Before
- Test your tech: camera, mic, internet, IDE
- Close all unnecessary tabs and notifications
- Have backup plan ready (phone hotspot, backup device)
- Take 5 deep breaths (box breathing: 4 in, 4 hold, 4 out, 4 hold)

### 5 Minutes Before
- Open a blank editor/IDE
- Have scratch paper and pen
- Remind yourself: "I'm prepared. I have a framework. Even if I get stuck, I know how to recover."
- Smile (it actually reduces cortisol)

## During Each Round — Quick Protocols

### Coding Round (45 min)
```
Min 0-2:   Greet warmly
Min 2-7:   UNDERSTAND (restate, clarify, examples)
Min 7-12:  APPROACH (brute force → optimize → state plan)
Min 12-30: CODE (talk while writing, clean code)
Min 30-37: VERIFY (trace through examples, edge cases)
Min 37-45: FOLLOW-UP (optimizations, testing, extensions)
```

**If stuck:** "Let me think about what data structure would help here..." (30 sec max silence)

### System Design Round (45 min)
```
Min 0-5:   REQUIREMENTS (scope, scale, constraints)
Min 5-7:   ESTIMATION (QPS, storage, bandwidth)
Min 7-22:  HIGH-LEVEL DESIGN (draw architecture, API, data flow)
Min 22-37: DEEP DIVE (interviewer picks area to explore)
Min 37-42: WRAP-UP (trade-offs, monitoring, missing pieces)
Min 42-45: YOUR QUESTIONS
```

**Key rule:** YOU drive the conversation. Don't wait to be asked "what next?"

### Behavioral Round (45 min)
```
Format: 5-7 questions, each answered in 2-3 minutes (STAR)

S: 2 sentences setting the scene
T: Your specific role/responsibility
A: What YOU did (60% of answer time)
R: Outcome with NUMBERS

End each answer: "Would you like me to go deeper on any part?"
```

**Key rule:** Use "I" not "we" for your contributions.

## Things to Say (Scripts for Tricky Moments)

### When you need time to think:
> "That's a great question. Let me take 10 seconds to organize my thoughts."

### When you realize your approach is wrong:
> "Actually, I realize this won't handle [case]. Let me reconsider. I think [new approach] would be better because..."

### When you don't understand the question:
> "Could you clarify what you mean by [specific part]? I want to make sure I'm solving the right problem."

### When given a hint:
> "Ah, that's helpful. So you're suggesting I think about [restate hint]. Yes, that makes sense because [reasoning]..."

### When you can't solve it optimally:
> "I have a working O(n²) solution. I believe the optimal is O(n) using [technique], but I'm not immediately seeing how. Would you like me to code my current solution, or should I keep working toward the optimal?"

### End of round — asking questions:
> "What's the most challenging technical problem your team solved recently?"
> "How does your team approach code reviews and knowledge sharing?"
> "What does a typical week look like for someone in this role?"

## Confidence Reminders

Read these before walking in:

1. **You prepared.** The framework is in your muscle memory.
2. **Getting stuck is normal.** Even FAANG employees get stuck. Recovery matters more than perfection.
3. **They WANT you to succeed.** Interviewers are rooting for you — a hire means their team gets stronger.
4. **It's a conversation, not an exam.** Think of it as pair programming with a colleague.
5. **One bad round won't kill you.** Most companies hire on the overall packet, not individual rounds.
6. **You can always ask for a hint.** Using a hint gracefully is better than staring blankly.
7. **If you don't get this one, there will be others.** This is practice for your career, not a life-or-death moment.

## Post-Interview

### Immediately After
- Write down every question asked (for future prep)
- Note what went well and what didn't
- Send thank-you email to recruiter (not interviewer directly unless given their email)

### Thank-You Template
```
Subject: Thank you — [Role] Interview

Hi [Recruiter Name],

Thank you for coordinating today's interviews. I really enjoyed the conversations, 
especially [specific thing — like "the system design discussion about scaling 
notification systems"].

I'm even more excited about the [role] after learning about [something specific]. 
Please let me know if there's anything else I can provide.

Best regards,
Prince Patel
```

### If You Don't Hear Back (1 week)
```
"Hi [Recruiter], I hope you're well. I wanted to follow up on my interviews 
from [date]. I'm still very interested in the role and would appreciate any 
update on the timeline. Thank you!"
```

## Emergency Troubleshooting

| Situation | Action |
|-----------|--------|
| Internet drops | Switch to phone hotspot immediately. Apologize briefly. |
| Complete blank on a question | "I haven't encountered this exact pattern. Let me think from first principles..." |
| Interviewer is cold/rude | Stay professional. Don't take it personally. Some interviewers are stressed. |
| You finish too early | "I've solved this in O(n). Want me to explore the O(1) space variant?" |
| Nervous/shaking | Hands under desk. Speak slower. Take a sip of water. |
| Previous round went badly | Each round is independent. Fresh slate. |

---

## The 3 Things That Matter Most

If you remember nothing else:

1. **Think out loud** — Silent = invisible thinking. Speak your reasoning.
2. **Start with brute force** — A working bad solution beats a perfect non-solution.
3. **Confirm before coding** — "Here's my approach. Shall I code it?" saves you from going down wrong paths.

Good luck. You've got this.
